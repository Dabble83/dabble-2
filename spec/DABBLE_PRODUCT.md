# Dabble Product Specification

## Product Description

Dabble is a calm, anti-hustle platform that connects people in real life through skills exchange and learning. Unlike marketplace platforms that commodify knowledge and encourage transactional relationships, Dabble creates space for genuine community connections based on curiosity, mutual support, and the joy of learning together. The platform is designed to feel more like a neighborhood bulletin board than a commercial marketplace—warm, inviting, and focused on real-world connections. Users share what they can teach, discover what they want to learn, and build relationships through in-person exchanges that happen in their actual communities.

## Key Concepts

### Dabblers
Dabblers are the core user type—people who are both learners and sharers. Every user is expected to have both "offers" (things they can help others with) and "wants" (things they want to learn). This dual identity prevents the platform from becoming a one-way service marketplace and reinforces the community exchange model. Dabblers are not "instructors" or "students"—they are peers who share knowledge with each other.

### Skills Exchange
The fundamental unit of interaction is skills exchange, not payment. While credits can be used as a helpful accounting mechanism for balance (especially useful when skills have different perceived values), the default mode is direct swaps: "I'll teach you sourdough if you help me fix my bike." Credits are optional, transparent, and never required—the platform works perfectly fine as a pure swap system.

### Credits (Optional)
Credits exist purely as a convenience tool to facilitate exchanges, not as a monetization feature. Users can optionally assign credit values to their skills, and the system can help match exchanges based on credit balance, but credits are never required, never charged for, and never bought. They are simply a way to say "this skill typically takes X hours" or "this exchange feels balanced at Y credits." Credits are community-set and transparent.

### Swaps Encouraged
The platform actively encourages direct swaps over credits. UI copy, matching algorithms, and community norms all favor the simplicity of "I'll teach you X, you teach me Y." Swaps build stronger relationships and reinforce the anti-commercial ethos. Credits are a fallback for complex multi-person exchanges, not the primary mode.

### Real-Life Connection
All exchanges are expected to happen in person, in real life, in actual neighborhoods. The platform includes location-based discovery (with privacy controls) so users can find dabblers nearby. Online-only or remote learning exchanges are not the focus—this is about building local community through face-to-face interaction.

### Privacy & Safety
Location sharing is opt-in and granular. Users can choose neighborhood-level precision (e.g., "Park Slope, Brooklyn") or approximate (e.g., "Brooklyn"). Exact addresses are never required or shared. All interactions are community-moderated, and users build reputation through successful exchanges. Safety features include profile verification, exchange history, and community reporting.

### Anti-Hustle Principles
- No monetization features (no payment processing, no subscription tiers, no featured listings)
- No marketplace language (no "buy/sell," "marketplace," "providers," "customers")
- No gamification (no badges, leaderboards, or achievement systems)
- No growth hacking (no viral sharing mechanics, referral bonuses, or network effects)
- No urgency tactics (no "limited time," "limited slots," or scarcity messaging)
- No commercial optimization (no A/B testing for conversion, no marketing funnels)

### Community First
The platform exists to serve existing communities and help new ones form. It is not trying to "scale" or "disrupt" anything. Success is measured by quality of connections, not user count or engagement metrics. The design should feel like it belongs in a community center, not a Silicon Valley startup.
